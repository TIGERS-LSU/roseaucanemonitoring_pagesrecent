Why Are Louisiana's Roseau Cane Marshes Disappearing?

Roseau cane — a tall, fast-growing wetland grass — has long been the backbone of Louisiana's coastal marshes. But over the past decade, vast stretches of this marsh have died off. Researchers at LSU's Wetland Plant Ecology Lab have spent years figuring out why, what it means for the coast, and what we can do about it.

What Is Roseau Cane and Why Does It Matter?

The Disappearing Backbone of Louisiana's Coast

Roseau cane (Phragmites australis) is a tall, dense wetland grass that has shaped Louisiana's coastal marshes for centuries. It holds riverbanks together, filters sediment from the water, builds up marsh soil, and provides essential habitat for birds, fish, alligators, and muskrats. In the lower Mississippi River Delta — where the river meets the Gulf of Mexico — entire landscapes are defined by its towering green stands.

Starting around 2012 and increasingly visible by 2016, large patches of this marsh began turning brown, then collapsing entirely into bare mudflats. Some areas that once supported 6-foot-tall stands of cane are now open water. This dieback is alarming because it removes the very plants that hold the land together — and Louisiana is already losing coastal land at an extraordinary rate.

Dr. Elsey-Quirk's team has been on the front lines of understanding this crisis — combining 16 years of monitoring data, controlled experiments, and ecological surveys to identify the causes and chart a path toward recovery.

What's Causing the Die-Off?

Sixteen Years of Tracking Louisiana's Marshes

To understand the dieback, researchers tapped into a statewide network of wetland monitoring stations — the Louisiana Coastwide Reference Monitoring System — that has been recording vegetation, water levels, and salinity at more than 70 marsh locations since 2007. By analyzing 16 years of this data, scientists could see not just what happened, but when things started going wrong and why some areas were hit harder than others.

The Breaking Point — A Perfect Storm in 2012

The actual die-off was triggered by an extreme drought in the summer of 2012 — one of the worst on record in the United States. When the Mississippi River ran low, saltwater from the Gulf crept far inland. Then Tropical Storm Debby hit, followed by Hurricane Isaac, which flooded the marshes with nearly three feet of saltwater. Plants that were already struggling from years of rising water had no reserves left.

What the Data Shows
The most affected areas experienced salt levels 4× higher than normal during the growing season of 2012 — far beyond what Roseau cane can survive without the soil becoming waterlogged.

The Slow Burn — Rising Seas Make Everything Worse

The drought was the spark, but the kindling had been building for years. Louisiana's coast is sinking while sea levels rise — meaning marshes spend more and more time underwater. Areas that died in 2012 were already sitting in water about 15 cm deeper on average than marshes that survived. More flooding means less oxygen in the soil, which lets harmful chemicals build up that ultimately poison the plant roots.

The Big Picture
Interestingly, some saltier marshes further west didn't experience dieback — because they had less standing water. This tells us flooding duration, not just salt, is the key factor that made some marshes vulnerable.

What Happens to the Soil When the Marsh Dies

Field researchers compared the soil and structure of healthy Roseau cane stands to areas that had experienced dieback. Most things looked similar on the surface — elevation, soil strength, and total plant root mass were all about the same. But one key difference stood out: healthy stands had more living underground stems (rhizomes), while dieback areas contained more decomposed, broken-down organic matter.

Even though total plant cover partially bounced back after the dieback — with other marsh plants filling in the gaps — the soil became less dense and lost some of its mineral content. This matters because denser, mineral-rich soils better hold the land in place against flooding and erosion. Recovery of the land itself may lag behind even as the green returns above the waterline.

Not All Roseau Cane Is the Same

Three Varieties, Three Different Stories

Roseau cane actually comes in several genetically distinct varieties — think of them like different breeds of the same plant. Three of them are common along the Louisiana Gulf Coast, and they behave quite differently under stress. Understanding which variety is tougher, which is spreading, and which is most at risk has major implications for how we approach marsh restoration.

To test this directly, researchers built "marsh organs" — rows of PVC pipes placed in the marsh at different water depths — and planted all three varieties across a range of flooding conditions at two sites: one with mostly fresh water near the lower Mississippi River, and one with brackish, saltier water further west. They tracked plant survival and growth for two full growing seasons.

Why Flooded Soil Becomes Deadly — The Underground Chemistry

One of the most important discoveries from the marsh organ experiments was how flooding kills these plants — it's not just the water itself. When soil is submerged too long without oxygen, bacteria take over and break down organic matter in ways that produce toxic byproducts. Think of it like a slow rot happening underground.

Two chemicals in particular were found to be the main culprits: acetic acid (the same compound found in vinegar) builds up in waterlogged soil and is the strongest predictor of plant death. Hydrogen sulfide (which smells like rotten eggs) accumulates especially in saltier conditions and can reach levels where virtually no plants survive. The longer the marsh stays flooded, the worse these chemicals get — creating a cycle that's very hard for plants to recover from.

Can the Marsh Come Back? Seeds, Pollen & Recovery

Studying How Roseau Cane Reproduces — and Whether It Can Bounce Back

One of the most urgent questions after a large-scale die-off is: can the plants come back on their own? Researchers surveyed 35 sites along the Mississippi River to examine how well each variety of Roseau cane produces seeds and pollen, and whether seeds in the soil can sprout new plants. The answers varied dramatically across the three varieties — with real consequences for what recovery looks like.

🌸
The European Variety — More Seeds, More Spread Risk
The European variety was the only one to produce seeds that actually sprout into new plants. Even more striking — when this variety was under stress at die-off sites, it produced even more pollen than it did in healthy conditions, as if ramping up reproduction to escape the threat. This is part of what makes it a successful invader in other parts of the country.
Why This Matters
Disturbed die-off areas could become prime spots for the European variety to move in and take over — displacing the native Delta type that Louisiana managers want to protect and restore. Monitoring for early arrival is important.
🌾
The Delta Variety — A Stress Response Without Seeds
Interestingly, Delta-type plants at die-off sites actually produced more seed heads than healthy plants — nearly double the number. But those seeds aren't viable; they won't grow. This appears to be a stress response — the plant "tries" harder to reproduce when threatened, but the seeds just don't develop. Recovery for Delta-type Roseau cane relies almost entirely on regrowth from underground roots.
Restoration Note
Because Delta-type spreads underground rather than by seed, restoration of this variety requires physically replanting — you can't just wait for it to seed in from neighboring areas. The presence of healthy living underground stems (rhizomes) is the best sign a marsh can recover on its own.
🚫
Will Different Varieties Crossbreed? Probably Not Here.
Scientists have been concerned that different varieties of Roseau cane might interbreed and produce new, potentially more invasive hybrids. The good news from the lower Mississippi Delta: the three varieties here flower at completely different times of year — the European type in June–July, the Delta and Gulf types in October–January — so they simply don't overlap. They also tend to grow in geographically separate areas. The risk of hybrid formation in this region appears very low.
🌱
What Grows Back After the Cane Disappears
Even though Roseau cane seeds essentially never sprout from the soil in natural conditions, many other plant species do. Scientists found 27 different species in soil samples from die-off areas — including arrowhead (Sagittaria), elephant ear (Colocasia), and bulrushes. These plants may not be Roseau cane, but they can help stabilize the marsh soil and slow land loss while Roseau cane slowly recovers through root regrowth.

Does Dieback-Affected Soil Prevent Recovery?

A reasonable worry was that the soil in die-off areas might be so degraded that even healthy plants couldn't get established there. Researchers tested this by growing the European variety in soil collected from both healthy and die-off sites. The result: no significant difference in germination or plant growth. The soil itself isn't the barrier — the problem is the flooding. As long as conditions in the lower delta remain heavily waterlogged, even freshly planted seedlings struggle to take hold. Raising the marsh surface is key before replanting can succeed.

What We've Learned — Key Takeaways

🧩
It Takes Two — One Crisis Isn't Enough
The 2012 drought alone wouldn't have killed so much marsh if the land hadn't already been slowly sinking and flooding more. It was the combination — years of gradual stress weakening the plants, then a sudden salt shock — that crossed the line. Prevent one and you reduce the damage from the other.
🔬
The Soil Turns Against the Plants
When marsh soil stays flooded too long, it becomes chemically hostile — producing vinegar-like acids and sulfur compounds that poison roots. This underground chemistry is one of the main reasons plants die, not just the water itself. Managing flood duration is as important as managing salt levels.
🧬
Variety Matters for Restoration
Not all Roseau cane handles stress the same way. The native Delta variety is the toughest in the harshest conditions and should be the focus of replanting efforts. The European variety's ability to spread by seed means open, disturbed areas need to be watched for its arrival.
🔄
The Marsh Partially Came Back — but Changed
After the major die-off, plant life did return to many areas — but it looked different. Roseau cane stayed low, and other plant species moved in. The number of plant species in the delta has dropped from around 12 before 2012 to just 7 or fewer today, and more flood-tolerant plants like elephant ear have taken over in some spots.
🏔️
The Ground Beneath Is Changing Too
Die-off areas showed subtle but important changes in soil structure — becoming less dense and losing mineral content. While the marsh surface elevation hasn't dramatically changed yet, these shifts could affect how well the land holds together over the long term, especially under continued sea-level rise.
⚠️
The Future Looks More Challenging
The trends are moving in the wrong direction. Louisiana's marshes are flooding more each decade, and extreme droughts — like the one in 2012 — are projected to become more common with climate change. With the ground continuing to sink, future salt intrusion events will hit a marsh that's even more vulnerable than it was in 2012.

What Can We Do About It?

Louisiana's coastal marshes are worth billions — they buffer communities from storms, support commercial fisheries, and protect shipping channels that move over $150 billion in goods every year. The research from the Wetland Plant Ecology Lab points directly to concrete actions that could help slow or reverse the losses.

⬆️

Build the Marsh Back Up — Literally

The single most important thing we can do is raise the marsh surface so it spends less time underwater. Since the land is sinking, we need to bring sediment in — either by pumping it from dredged material, directing river sediment into marshes through diversions, or applying thin layers of sediment strategically. Less flooding means less chemical buildup underground, and plants that can actually survive long enough to spread.

🌿

Plant the Right Roseau Cane in the Right Places

When replanting die-off areas, use the native Delta variety — it's the most resilient to the salty, flooded conditions of the lower delta. But don't expect plants to survive if the water is still too deep; raise the land first, then plant. Restoration trials confirm that Delta-type Roseau cane has low survival in areas with consistently deep standing water.

🛡️

Watch for the European Variety Spreading Into Disturbed Areas

Die-off areas with bare or recovering ground are exactly the kind of disturbed habitat where the invasive European variety can move in by seed and outcompete native plants. Restoration projects should include regular surveys to detect its presence early — before it establishes and displaces what's being carefully restored.

💧

Keep Fresh Water Flowing to the Delta

The 2012 die-off was triggered largely because low river flow let saltwater creep too far inland. River management decisions — how deep shipping channels are dredged, how much water is diverted, how dams are operated upstream — all influence how much freshwater reaches the delta during dry periods. Maintaining freshwater flow during the spring and early summer growing season is especially critical for marsh health.

🔬

Use Nature's Tools to Control Invasive Varieties Elsewhere

In parts of the country where Roseau cane is an unwanted invasive (like the Great Lakes or Atlantic coast), this research suggests that strategic flooding and mowing may be effective and environmentally gentler alternatives to herbicides. Since prolonged flooding combined with soil oxygen depletion is what kills these plants, water management can be a powerful control tool.

Nature Communications · 2024 · Open Access
Vegetation dieback in the Mississippi River Delta triggered by acute drought and chronic relative sea-level rise
Tracy Elsey-Quirk, Austin Lynn, Michael Derek Jacobs, Rodrigo Diaz, James T. Cronin, Lixia Wang, Haosheng Huang & Dubravko Justic
The main study behind this research page. Using 16 years of monitoring data from across coastal Louisiana, this paper shows that the large Roseau cane die-off beginning in 2012 was set off by a historic drought pushing saltwater into fresh marshes — but was made far worse because those marshes had already been slowly flooding more due to sinking land and rising seas. Roseau cane cover dropped by 68% on average over the study period, and recovery was slow and incomplete in the most flooded areas. The paper identifies conditions under which marshes decline, partially recover, or never come back.

Return of the Phrag-i: evaluating sexual reproduction mechanisms amenable to dieback recovery and potential invasiveness across Phragmites australis haplotypes
Olivia Hurley, Austin Lynn, Aaron DeVries, Christopher Reid & Tracy Elsey-Quirk
Can Roseau cane recover by growing new plants from seeds? This study surveyed 35 sites along the Mississippi River to find out. The answer depends entirely on which variety you're looking at — the European variety can produce viable seeds and is the only one that successfully sprouts new seedlings. The native Delta and Gulf varieties essentially cannot recover this way. Hybridization between varieties is also very unlikely here due to their different flowering seasons. These findings help explain why recovery in Louisiana is so slow, and why the invasive European variety poses a spreading risk in disturbed areas.

There's Something in the Porewater: Investigating Abiotic Factors Associated with Phragmites australis Dieback
Austin Lynn, Tracy Quirk — Wetland Plant Ecology Lab, LSU
Presented at a national wetlands conference, this work combined the flooding experiments with an analysis of real-world monitoring data from Louisiana marshes. It showed that dieback sites experience significantly more frequent high-salinity events than healthy sites — about 18% of months at dieback sites had very high salt levels, compared to just 4.5% at healthy sites nearby. This confirmed that the flooding-plus-salt combination seen in the lab experiment is exactly what's playing out across real Louisiana marshes.
